# Place Editorial Style

## Editorial objective

Do not tell the reader everything about the place. Tell them enough to understand the experience they could have there.

## What every entry should answer

- Why would I go?
- What kind of occasion is it for?
- What does the room feel like?
- Who tends to use it?
- Can I get in?
- What will I spend?
- What should I order?
- What is around it?
- How does it change through time?

## Headline style

Headlines should identify a place through a useful and memorable truth.

Examples:

- The bar for one last drink in SoHo
- A West Village martini without the ceremony
- Where Williamsburg goes when dinner ends
- A room built for talking, until about nine
- The rare Midtown bar worth arriving early for
- Where to watch the match without surrendering the evening

## Headline rules

- Use sentence case.
- Make one claim.
- Ground the claim in experience.
- Prefer situations and verbs over adjectives.
- Use neighborhood names when geography adds meaning.
- Avoid "best," "ultimate," "hottest" and listicle language.

## Place description structure

Recommended 100–180 words:

1. State what the place is for.
2. Describe arrival.
3. Describe the room and social arrangement.
4. Explain how it changes over the evening.
5. Give one clear recommendation or caution.
6. Connect it to the surrounding area.

## Formal data language

Use:

- high-spend nightlife;
- budget-friendly concentration;
- student-heavy at night;
- visitor-heavy;
- late-night intensity;
- polished nightlife;
- neighborhood-oriented.

Avoid using slang such as "bougie" or "college-kid bar" as canonical data.
